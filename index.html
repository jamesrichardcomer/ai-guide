Intent: What Do You Actually Want?

Sounds simple. It's not. What you want changes as you use it. You ask for something, get it, realize that's not quite right, refine, and repeat. Sometimes you don't even know what you want until you try to explain it.

How to get your intent out

Talk, don't type. I use Wispr Flow ($15/month) for speech-to-text. Last month I transcribed around 120,000 words. Clunky, but way faster than typing everything out. Claude also has built-in transcription.

Brain dump while driving. Instead of one prompt at a time, just get a huge amount out at once. You drive a lot. That's your time.

Context: The Boring Part That Matters Most

Context is just a grind. Boring, takes forever, but probably the most important thing you can do. It comes from two places: stuff on your computer and stuff in your head.

Start with files. Put spreadsheets, Word docs, CSVs, whatever into a folder. Point Co-Work at that folder. Done. For you, that could be daily field reports, production data, well completion logs, whatever you already have sitting on your computer.

External data needs MCPs. If your data lives somewhere else (email, databases, bank accounts), you need what they call MCPs. Think of these as API connections between the AI and your other services. I have MCPs for my email (both Outlook and Gmail), my bank accounts through SimpleFin ($1.50/month), Apple Reminders and Notes, my calendar, text messages, and my browser.

Setting up MCPs can get technical. This is where Claude Code might come in, even if Co-Work is your main tool.

A real example: I used to manually export transactions from Bank of America, Chase, Fidelity, and Discover. It took about 30 minutes. Now SimpleFin pulls all of it in 3 seconds. $1.50/month.

This is the harder part. You know a ton about your industry, your workflows, your preferences, and almost none of it is written down anywhere. Getting it out takes time. I'm still working on it months later.

For your work, a lot of what matters is stuff these models just don't know. Completion processes, well specs, field terminology, how your team actually operates. If you write that up and drop it in your folder, it goes from generic answers to actually useful.

I get my context out by brain dumping into the notes app while driving. You drive a lot. That's probably your best approach.

Context is just grunt work, but it's what makes everything else possible. Get your files in a folder, get your data connected if you can, and start getting the stuff in your head written down somewhere.

Execution: Getting It to Do What You Want

You have your intent, you have your context, and now you're tinkering. You're figuring out how to get it to do the actual work the way you want it done.

It can be tedious. You'll say "no, not like that" a bunch of times. But once you nail a repeatable workflow, it pays for itself quickly.

It took me a while to get this right. Now it handles my font, my signature, attachments, and creates drafts in the correct account. It still has some bugs. It doesn't always reply to the exact thread I want, and it can't schedule send. But it does enough that I just copy, paste into the thread, and send.

Getting there was a process. But every email I draft now is faster because I went through that process once.

I built a workflow called "Sync" that reads my Reminders, Notes, email, and calendar, figures out where each item belongs, and asks me to approve or skip. I also have one called "Productive" that looks for tasks I've flagged with a robot emoji (meaning "Claude should handle this") and just does them. I review the results.

The pattern

Anything you do repeatedly has a huge return on investment if you can get it set up correctly. Even if it takes a few hours to build the workflow, if you do the task every week, you break even fast.

What Works

What It Can and Can't Do

Here's what I've actually found.

✔

Works Well

  • Anything with verifiable outcomes (did the numbers add up? did the email draft correctly?)
  • Common tasks that lots of people do (email, data formatting, document cleanup)
  • Structured, mechanical work (reformatting spreadsheets, file organization)
  • Research and summarization
  • Following specific, detailed instructions
⚠

Watch Out

  • Niche, specialized analysis where it can't verify its own work
  • Vague prompts (if you don't know what you want, it definitely won't)
  • Complex visualizations and charts
  • Creative or analytical work requiring judgment calls
  • Anything where the "right answer" is subjective and specific to you

The broad pattern: these programs can basically do whatever you want if you explain yourself well enough and give them all the context. The bottleneck is usually you, not the AI.

Every free moment where I'm not dealing with the baby, I'm on my computer building something. It takes my attention in a way that's hard to explain.

Getting Started

Where I'd Start If I Were You

You don't need to do all of this at once. I've been at it for about a month and I'm still building. Here's what I'd do in your shoes.

1

Open Co-Work and point it at a folder

Pick a folder with some of your spreadsheets or documents. Production data, daily reports, cost sheets, whatever. Doesn't need to be organized. Just give it something to work with.

2

Start with something simple

Give it a spreadsheet and say "clean this up" or "make a summary of this data." You could hand it a messy well log and say "pull out the key completions data and put it in a clean table." See what happens.

3

Get your context out while you drive

Talk into your notes app about your work. What do you do every day? What data do you deal with? What's tedious? What do you wish someone else would just handle? You drive a lot. That's your time to do this.

4

Build from there

Once you see what it can do with your files, you'll start having ideas. Maybe you want it to pull data from your email, or run a query against a database, or build a weekly summary from your daily field reports. That's when MCPs and more advanced setups come in.

5

Be patient with the tinkering

It's going to frustrate you sometimes. You'll ask for something and it won't get it right. That's part of the process. The repeatable workflows you build through that tinkering are where the real value shows up over time.

One more thing

If you ever get stuck tinkering with something and it's just not working, step away and brain dump about the problem. Talk about why it sucks, what you wish would be better, what you've tried. Get a bunch of context out at once instead of going one prompt at a time. I do this all the time while commuting. It works way better than sitting there going back and forth, prompt by prompt.
